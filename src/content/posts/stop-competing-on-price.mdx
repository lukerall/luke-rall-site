Most freelancers lose deals before they even get to the pitch. Not because their work isn't good enough — because they walk into the room already thinking about how low they can go.

That was me at the start. I'd hear a client hesitate and immediately start adjusting my price in my head. "Maybe if I drop 20% they'll say yes." It felt like strategy. It wasn't. It was just fear wearing a business hat.

Here's what I learned: price is almost never the actual objection. What they're really saying is that they're not yet sure you can deliver. Fix that — and the price conversation disappears.

## The Race to the Bottom

When you compete on price, you're choosing to play a game you will eventually lose. There will always be someone willing to do it cheaper. Always. Especially in content creation, design, marketing — wherever the barrier to entry is low, the race to the bottom is crowded.

I watched other freelancers in my space undercutting each other into unsustainable rates. Working more, earning less, resenting clients. That's not a business — that's a trap with a logo.

The moment I decided I wasn't going to compete on price was the moment my sales conversations changed completely.

## What High-Ticket Sales Actually Looks Like

Leading with price is backwards. The client's first question shouldn't be "how much?" — it should be "can this person actually do what I need?"

Your job in a sales conversation is to make the answer to that second question undeniably yes before the price ever comes up.

How you do that:

**Lead with past work.** Not just a portfolio link — walk them through a real case. "We worked with a client in X industry, they had Y problem, here's what we built, here's what changed for them." Specifics build credibility faster than anything else.

**Mirror their language.** If they say "we want to look more established," don't pitch content — pitch authority. Match what they're worried about, not what you think they need.

**Make the ask confident.** When you arrive at price, say it once, clearly, and don't immediately explain or justify it. Hesitation signals that even you aren't sure it's worth it. Confidence communicates that you've had this conversation before and the number is fair.

## The Steel Construction Client

I closed a retainer with a lightweight steel construction firm that was looking to expand internationally. They were a solid, credible business — but their digital presence didn't reflect that. The gap between who they were and how they looked online was costing them opportunities.

They weren't shopping around for the cheapest web designer. They were trying to solve a business problem: looking like the company they were becoming. When I reframed the conversation around that outcome — not deliverables, not hours, not packages — the price became a footnote.

They asked what it would cost. I told them. They said yes.

The reason it worked wasn't that the price was low. It was that by the time we got to price, they were already sold on the outcome.

## Price Is Not the Objection

When a prospect says "that's a bit out of our budget," here's what they're usually actually saying:

- I'm not convinced the ROI is there yet
- I don't fully trust that you'll deliver
- I haven't seen enough proof

None of those are solved by discounting. All of them are solved by demonstrating value more clearly.

Go back and ask: "What would need to be true for this to be an easy yes?" You'll almost always get something useful — an objection you can actually address.

## The Shift

This isn't complicated, but it does require you to believe that your work is worth full price. That's the harder part for most people. Impostor syndrome pushes you toward discounting because it feels safer — you're pre-empting the rejection.

But the client doesn't know your internal doubts. They only see how you show up. Show up like someone who charges this rate because they've earned it — and most of the time, they'll believe you.

Stop adjusting the number. Start improving the pitch.
